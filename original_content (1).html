About Lentil Soup

A Delicious & Comforting Meal

Discover a delightful and hearty lentil soup crafted with a blend of fresh vegetables including carrots, onions, celery, and garlic. The aromatic spices—cumin, coriander, and turmeric—enhance the flavors, while a handful of fresh spinach is added at the end to infuse additional nutrients.

This comforting meal is perfect for dining during the cooler months, offering warmth and nourishment in every spoonful.

Why This Meal Matters

This lentil soup is more than just a meal—it's a treasure trove of memories and traditions. Reminiscent of family gatherings and cherished moments at home, the soup embodies comfort and simplicity.

Embracing a vegan lifestyle has further spotlighted its significance, proving that delicious, wholesome, and easy-to-make dishes can ignite both excitement and satisfaction.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup dried green or brown lentils
  • 1 medium onion, diced
  • 2 carrots, diced
  • 2 celery stalks, diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tablespoon olive oil
  • 1 teaspoon ground cumin
  • 1 teaspoon ground coriander
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground turmeric
  • 4 cups vegetable broth
  • 2 cups water
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 2 cups fresh spinach (optional)

Detailed Nutritional & Epigenetic Analysis

GMO Status

Are there any components of your meal that may include a genetically modified food (GMO)?

No, none of the ingredients in this meal are genetically modified. According to the USDA's Bioengineered Food List, common GMO foods include soy, corn, canola, and certain squashes. Since this meal consists of lentils, vegetables, and spices, none of the components are bioengineered or commonly genetically modified.

Epigenetic Effects

What components of your meal may alter your epigenome?

Carbon-donating nutrients (Methylation Supporters)

  • Lentils and spinach are rich in folate (vitamin B9), which is a key methyl donor in DNA methylation.
  • Garlic and onions contain choline, which also contributes to methylation and can support proper gene regulation.

Histone Deacetylase (HDAC) Inhibitors (Help keep genes active)

  • Turmeric (curcumin) is a known HDAC inhibitor, meaning it can help turn on beneficial genes involved in inflammation reduction and disease prevention.
  • Garlic contains sulfur compounds that may have epigenetic effects, influencing detoxification pathways and immune function.
  • Spinach and carrots are high in polyphenols and flavonoids, which have been linked to epigenetic modifications that promote health.

This meal supports healthy epigenetic regulation through methylation and HDAC inhibition, which may positively influence gene expression related to inflammation, metabolism, and detoxification.

Protein Content

What are the sources of protein in your meal? Would you consider this meal high in protein and why or why not?

  • The primary protein source in this meal is lentils, which provide 18g of protein per cup.
  • Spinach also contributes a small amount of protein (0.9g per cup).

I would consider that this meal is relatively high in protein, especially for a plant-based meal, since it provides around 20g of protein per serving, which is 40% of the recommended daily intake (RDI) for protein.

Why is it high in protein?

  • It provides a complete range of essential amino acids when combined with other plant foods throughout the day.
  • Lentils are a dense source of plant protein and fiber.

Amino Acid Processing

If you eat more amino acids than your body is going to use, what happens to them?

When the body has excess amino acids, it cannot store them like fats or carbohydrates.

  1. Deamination: The body removes the amino group (NH₂) from excess amino acids.
  2. Urea Cycle: The amino group is converted into urea in the liver and excreted through the urine.
  3. Energy Production: The remaining carbon skeleton is used for glucose (gluconeogenesis) or stored as fat if not immediately needed.

Excess protein does not get stored as protein; it's either used for energy or converted into fat if intake exceeds energy demands.

If your body needs a specific non-essential amino acid (say alanine, for example), but your meal doesn't include it, how can your body create this amino acid?

The body can synthesize non-essential amino acids through transamination, which involves...

  1. Using an existing amino acid: The body transfers an amino group from one amino acid (e.g., glutamate) to a keto acid (e.g., pyruvate).
  2. Creating the needed amino acid: This reaction produces the non-essential amino acid, such as alanine from pyruvate.

From what I understand, as long as the body has enough nitrogen and precursor molecules, it can synthesize most non-essential amino acids, making dietary intake of these specific amino acids unnecessary.

Epigenetics: How Lentil Soup Changes Your Genes

Did you know that the ingredients in our lentil soup can actually influence how your genes work? It's not science fiction—it's epigenetics!

What is Epigenetics?

Epigenetics is the study of how your behaviors and environment can cause changes that affect the way your genes work. Unlike genetic changes, epigenetic changes are reversible and do not change your DNA sequence.

Think of it this way: if your DNA is like a cookbook, epigenetics is like the chef deciding which recipes to make, how much of each dish to prepare, and when to serve them.

How Food Affects Your Epigenome

The nutrients in your food can influence which genes are turned on or off. This happens through processes like DNA methylation and histone modification.

Our lentil soup is specially designed to support healthy epigenetic regulation, potentially influencing gene expression related to inflammation, metabolism, and detoxification.
